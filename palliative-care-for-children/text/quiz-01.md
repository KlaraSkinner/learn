1.  Why are the palliative care needs of children different to those of adults?

<!-- -->

1.  Adults only need palliative care at the end-of-life

2.  Adults need to make all the decisions for the child

3.  Children are not ‘small adults’ as their developmental needs are different

4.  Children can only receive palliative care in a hospital setting

<!-- -->

1.  It is for children with cancer and HIV/AIDS only

2.  Children need palliative care from time of diagnosis of a life-limiting or life-threatening condition

3.  It can be provided in any setting

4.  It aims to relieve pain and suffering

1.  The Constitution of South Africa states that:

<!-- -->

1.  Only adults have the right to basic health services which includes curative and palliative care

2.  Only adults over the age of 18 years have the right to social services

3.  Every child has the right to basic healthcare services which includes palliative care

<!-- -->

1.  What is a continuum of care?

<!-- -->

1.  The care and support that is provided throughout the course of an illness

2.  The care that is provided once the disease cannot be treated anymore

3.  Support for the patient when their child is admitted into hospital until they are discharged

4.  Support that is provided to the family after the patient has died

<!-- -->

1.  When should palliative care be offered?

<!-- -->

1.  When the doctor refers the child to a hospice

2.  After the doctor has informed the family that curative treatment is no longer feasible

3.  Alongside curative care from the time of diagnosis of a life-threatening or life-limiting illness

4.  To children whose family can afford palliative care services

<!-- -->

1.  An illness trajectory:

    1.  Tells you exactly when the patient will die

    2.  Is the general course an illness is likely to follow

    3.  Will indicate when active treatment needs to be started

    4.  Follows the same pattern in both adults and children

2.  An illness trajectory can be helpful to:

<!-- -->

1.  Plan the management goals and the changing needs along the course of the illness

2.  Estimate the cost of palliative care

3.  Decide when to stop educating the child

4.  Make arrangements to provide private nursing care

<!-- -->

1.  Categorising children’s illnesses can assist in:

<!-- -->

1.  Deciding what medication to treat the child with

2.  Identifying the child’s palliative care needs

3.  Deciding which hospital to treat the child in

4.  Deciding when to give up hope

<!-- -->

1.  Children’s palliative care category 4 are conditions or illnesses that are:

<!-- -->

1.  Progressive conditions that cannot be cured

2.  Life-threatening for which curative treatment may be possible but can fail, such as cancer

3.  Likely to end in premature death but where long periods of treatment can prolong life, such as an HIV infected child on treatment

4.  Irreversible but non-progressive, such as severe cerebral palsy

<!-- -->

1.  What does perinatal palliative care focus on?

<!-- -->

1.  Decision making to terminate the pregnancy if a life-limiting diagnosis is made during a routine antenatal ultrasound

2.  Guiding and supporting families in decision making and planning in the event of a life-limiting diagnosis being made during pregnancy or soon after birth.

3.  Good medical care for a newborn infant born with severe physical abnormalities

4.  Getting the mother home after the delivery of her infant

2.  Only at a hospital with special palliative care beds

3.  Only at home by a hospice service when the child is dying

4.  In any setting

1.  What is a hospice?

<!-- -->

1.  An organisation that provides palliative care in a variety of settings

2.  A small hospital found in a rural area

3.  A group of people who provide bereavement counselling

4.  A hospital for children with cancer
